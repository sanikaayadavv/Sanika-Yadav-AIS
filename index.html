Welcome to Kutaisi, Georgia — Your Next Unforgettable Adventure!

Stepping off the marshrutka (minibus) in Kutaisi, I realized I’d discovered Georgia’s best-kept secret. This city doesn’t announce itself with grandeur—it whispers through crumbling medieval facades, hums with the warmth of locals offering me unexpected invitations to supra (the famous Georgian feast), and captivates visitors through authentic experiences rather than polished tourism. I wasn’t prepared for how deeply this ancient capital would embed itself into my heart, or how vastly different it would feel from Georgia’s more well-trodden paths. Welcome to Kutaisi, where you’ll encounter thousand-year-old legends still breathing life into cobblestone streets, Byzantine architectural masterpieces that challenge your sense of time, and a food culture so intertwined with community that every meal becomes a window into Georgian soul.

The moment my eyes landed on the Colchis Fountain dominating the central roundabout—with its gilded bronze horses and mythological figures catching the Caucasus sunlight—I understood something profound: this wasn’t just another Caucasian city on the tourist circuit. This was Georgia’s second-largest metropolis, reimagined as a living museum where Soviet-era charm collides beautifully with medieval spirituality and contemporary vibrancy. What truly amazed me was discovering that Kutaisi served as Georgia’s capital from the 6th to 13th centuries, making it genuinely older and steeped in more historical significance than many European capitals tourists flock to yearly. The city’s population of around 147,000 people maintains a refreshingly unhurried pace—the kind of rhythm that allows you to genuinely connect with places and people rather than simply tick boxes on a checklist.

Here’s what makes Kutaisi genuinely different from Tbilisi’s crowded tourism scene or Batumi’s beach resort mentality: this is where authentic meets adventurous, where budget-friendly merges seamlessly with luxury, and where three-week itineraries compete with three-day escapes. I discovered that you can explore a UNESCO World Heritage monastery in the morning, navigate Soviet cable cars that haven’t changed since 1961 in the afternoon, discover underground cave systems that rival anything in Europe, feast on regional Georgian specialties that your taste buds have never experienced, and still have budget remaining for three more nights. Kutaisi doesn’t require you to choose between experiences—it generously offers you the freedom to chase legends, modern adventures, culinary thrills, and spiritual awakening simultaneously.

Whether you’re a budget backpacker maxing out value on every lari (Georgian currency), a luxury traveler seeking boutique experiences off the beaten path, a history enthusiast hungry for medieval Georgian architecture, or an adventure seeker ready to rappel through canyons and explore caves, Kutaisi welcomes you with open arms and competitive pricing that makes you wonder why every travel magazine hasn’t featured this city on their front cover yet.

Getting to Kutaisi: Your Gateway to Western Georgia Transportation Options to Kutaisi

Reaching Kutaisi from Tbilisi is straightforward and affordable—I tested multiple options and here’s what I discovered.

By Train (My Top Choice): Taking the train from Tbilisi Central Station to Rioni Station was my favorite journey. The train takes approximately 3.5 to 4 hours, costs between 14-26 GEL ($5-10 USD), and operates 3 services daily. The views of Georgia’s lush western landscape unfold gradually, giving you time to process the transition. Pro tip: Book through the Georgian Railways website or the alternative booking site if the official one acts up. From Rioni Station, catch bus #3 (40 tetri/0.4 GEL) to reach the city center, or grab a Bolt ride (around 8-10 GEL/$3-4 USD).

By Marshrutka Van (Budget Option): These minibuses depart from Kutaisi Central Station every 30 minutes starting from 6 AM and cost 20 GEL ($7-8 USD) for the 4-hour journey. They’re more frequent than trains, less formal, and genuinely entertaining if you enjoy local transportation theater. Marshrutkas typically leave when full rather than on fixed schedules, so budget extra time.

By Shared Taxi: If you’re traveling with companions or want more comfort, shared taxis cost 45-55 GEL per person for four passengers and take roughly the same time as marshrutkas.

By Rental Car: Through platforms like GetRentacar (use the code from their website for the best rates—starting from 35 USD/day), you’ll gain flexibility. The drive takes approximately 3 hours and reveals stunning countryside. You’ll need an International Driving Permit alongside your home country’s license.

Airport Transfer: If arriving at Kutaisi International Airport, located 25 kilometers from the city center, catch bus #101 every 30 minutes for 5 GEL ($1.75 USD), or book through Welcome Pickups for reliable, English-speaking drivers at transparent pricing.

Pro Transportation Tips for Getting Around Kutaisi

Once you’re in Kutaisi, getting around is refreshingly simple. City buses cost 60 tetri (approximately 20 cents USD) per ride. Marshrutkas (small minibuses operating fixed routes) cost 1-1.5 GEL. Use the Yandex Maps app to navigate routes—it’s more accurate than Google Maps for Georgian public transport.

The most efficient way to explore is by combining walking with occasional taxi rides. Bolt (Georgia’s ride-sharing app similar to Uber) charges 5-10 GEL ($2-4 USD) for most in-city trips. Street taxis exist but require negotiating fares beforehand to avoid overcharging—Bolt’s app-based system guarantees transparency.
